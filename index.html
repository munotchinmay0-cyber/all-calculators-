Understanding SIP & Compounding

What is an SIP?

A Systematic Investment Plan allows you to invest a small, fixed sum regularly in mutual funds or equity pools. Rather than timing the markets, you benefit from rupee-cost averaging and compounding over decades.

The Step-Up Advantage

An annual step-up SIP increases your contributions as your income grows. By stepping up investments by just 10% annually, you can easily double your final wealth compared to keeping investments flat.

Why Adjust for Inflation?

Purchasing power erodes over time. Adjusting for a 6% inflation rate gives you a realistic picture of what your future wealth will actually buy in today's terms, ensuring you don't undersave.

Understanding SWP & Wealth Sustainability

What is an SWP?

A Systematic Withdrawal Plan is the reverse of an SIP. While SIPs help build capital, an SWP allows you to systematically extract monthly income from an accumulated fund (like a retirement pool) while the remaining corpus continues to compound.

The Exhaustion Point

If the rate at which you withdraw capital (plus inflation increases) exceeds the rate of return earned by the underlying mutual fund, the corpus will enter a downward spiral. Our analyzer pinpoints exactly when this happens.

Inflation & Lifestyle Step-Up

In retirement, withdrawing a flat amount of money is unrealistic because living costs rise. Adjusting for 6% inflation means your ₹50k/month withdrawal becomes ₹90k/month in 10 years just to buy the same groceries.

Lumpsum Investment Mechanics

What is Lumpsum?

Lumpsum investment involves depositing a substantial amount of capital in a single installment, rather than spreading payments over time. This exposes the entire corpus to compound interest immediately.

Power of Time

In compound interest, time is your greatest asset. An initial ₹1 Lakh invested at 12% returns will double every 6 years. Over a 30-year period, it grows into roughly ₹30 Lakhs, demonstrating exponential curve shapes.

Nominal vs. Real Returns

Nominal return is the absolute growth percentage. Real return is the growth adjusted for inflation. Since price indexes increase annually, your nominal future sum is deflated by the inflation coefficient to evaluate true purchasing power.

Understanding Debt Mechanics

Indirect Cost of Debt

Borrowing money comes with secondary financing costs. For a long-term loan (e.g. 20 years at 9%), the total interest paid often exceeds the original principal borrowed. Showing this as a multiple (e.g., 2.1x) helps evaluate true debt burden.

Study Break (Grace Period) Costs

Most student loans offer a study break (grace period) during college. However, interest continues to build up and compound monthly during this time. This interest is added to your original loan balance, which inflates your starting EMI when repayments begin.

Power of Extra Payments

Loan schedules weigh interest payments heavily at the start. Paying a bit extra each month goes straight to reducing your main balance. This cuts down the interest you pay overall and lets you clear the loan years ahead of schedule.

Strategic Goal Engineering

Backward Induction Solving

Instead of guessing how much money you will end up with, the Goal Planner works backward. You set the target amount, and our engine resolves the precise starting contribution needed to achieve it given expected parameters.

Goal Inflation Deflection

Planning for ₹1 Crore in 20 years without adjusting for inflation is a major error. If inflation remains at 6%, you actually require ₹3.2 Crore in nominal terms to retain the purchasing power of ₹1 Crore today. The Planner handles this calculation seamlessly.

Dynamic Target Matching

Enabling annual savings step-up means you start with a much smaller monthly commitment today when your salary is lower, scaling it up annually to match standard corporate salary hikes and bonuses.
